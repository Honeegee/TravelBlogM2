About Bohol

Bohol, a heart-shaped island province in the Central Visayas region of the Philippines, is a treasure trove of natural wonders and historical heritage. Known for its unique combination of natural attractions, rich history, and vibrant culture, Bohol has become one of the Philippines' most popular tourist destinations.

The province consists of Bohol Island and 75 smaller surrounding islands, including the popular tourist destination of Panglao. What makes Bohol truly special is its diverse landscape, from the mysterious Chocolate Hills to its pristine beaches, from dense mahogany forests to historic colonial churches.

The island's most famous landmark, the Chocolate Hills, consists of at least 1,268 perfectly cone-shaped hills spread across several municipalities. These hills, which turn chocolate-brown during the dry season, have become an iconic symbol of Philippine tourism and are considered a National Geological Monument.

Bohol is also a crucial sanctuary for the Philippine Tarsier, one of the world's smallest primates. These nocturnal creatures, with their distinctive large eyes, have become ambassadors for wildlife conservation in the Philippines. The Tarsier Sanctuary in Corella provides a protected natural habitat for these endangered species.

The province has a rich historical heritage, evident in its colonial-era churches, watchtowers, and ancestral houses. The Blood Compact Site commemorates the first treaty of friendship between the Filipinos and Spaniards, marking a significant moment in Philippine history.

Top Attractions

  • Chocolate Hills - A geological wonder featuring over 1,000 symmetrical hills that turn brown during the dry season. This unique landscape is one of the most iconic natural landmarks in the Philippines, drawing tourists from around the world.
  • Tarsier Sanctuary - A conservation area where visitors can observe the endangered Philippine Tarsier in its natural habitat. These small primates are known for their large eyes and nocturnal habits, making the sanctuary a must-see for wildlife enthusiasts.
  • Panglao Island - Famous for its white-sand beaches, diving spots, and vibrant marine life. Panglao is a favorite destination for beach lovers and divers alike, offering beautiful coastal views and an array of underwater adventures.
  • Loboc River - Known for its scenic river cruises and floating restaurants offering local cuisine. A Loboc River cruise provides a relaxing way to enjoy the lush countryside, with local musicians often serenading passengers along the way.
  • Baclayon Church - One of the oldest stone churches in Asia, built during the Spanish colonial period. The church stands as a testament to Bohol’s rich religious and colonial history, showcasing centuries-old architecture and artifacts.
  • Man-made Forest - A two-kilometer stretch of densely planted mahogany trees creating a unique forest tunnel. This green canopy is a photographer's dream and provides a cool, refreshing stopover for travelers en route to the Chocolate Hills.
  • Hinagdanan Cave - A naturally lit cave with a crystal-clear underground pool. The stalactites and stalagmites within Hinagdanan Cave, combined with the sunlight filtering in, create an ethereal beauty that attracts both local and foreign tourists.
  • Blood Compact Site - A historical landmark commemorating the first treaty of friendship between Filipinos and Spaniards. This symbolic site marks a significant event in Philippine history and is a popular cultural attraction in Bohol.
  • Balicasag Island - A marine sanctuary known for excellent snorkeling and diving opportunities. The rich biodiversity around Balicasag Island makes it a prime spot for observing coral reefs and marine life, including sea turtles and colorful fish.
  • Beaches - Alona Beach, Dumaluan Beach, and Doljo Beach offer various water activities and relaxation spots. These beaches are perfect for sunbathing, swimming, and experiencing the laid-back island vibe of Panglao.

Practical Travel Information

Getting There: Bohol is accessible by air and sea. The new Bohol-Panglao International Airport serves as the main gateway, with regular flights from Manila, Cebu, and other major cities. Multiple airlines including Philippine Airlines, Cebu Pacific, and AirAsia operate daily flights. For those preferring sea travel, several ferry companies operate regular trips from Cebu City to Tagbilaran Port.

Within Bohol, transportation options include rental cars, motorcycles, tricycles, and organized tours. Many tourists opt for organized day tours that cover the major attractions, while others prefer the flexibility of hired vehicles or motorcycles for independent exploration.

Best Time to Visit: Bohol enjoys a tropical climate with two distinct seasons. The best time to visit is during the dry season from December to May, when weather conditions are ideal for outdoor activities and island hopping. The peak tourist season falls between December and February when the weather is cooler and perfect for sightseeing.

The rainy season runs from June to November, with occasional typhoons possible. While rainfall can affect outdoor activities, this period offers lower rates and fewer crowds. The Chocolate Hills maintain their appeal year-round, though they are most "chocolate-colored" during the dry season.

Local Cuisine: Bohol's culinary scene offers a delightful mix of Filipino classics and local specialties. Must-try dishes include *Bohol Chicken Binakol* (chicken soup cooked in coconut), *Calamay* (sticky rice delicacy), and fresh seafood from Panglao's restaurants.

The famous Loboc River Cruise combines sightseeing with dining, offering a buffet of Filipino dishes while floating down the scenic river. Local delicacies like *peanut kisses* (peanut-shaped cookies) and *broa* (local cookie made from coconut) make perfect souvenirs.
